Gli esami di maturità

In Italy the exams at the end of high school are called esami di Stato, but they are commonly known as esami di maturità (or simply as maturità). They represent a particular moment in the life of every student, because for those who want to continue studying they mark the passage between high school and university. They also represent the last school experience for those who choose to begin working. After maturità, the students receive a diploma, which is the formal recognition of their secondary education.

The exams take place over four days: there are three written assessments (prove scritte) and an oral exam (orale). The first written assessment is an essay in Italian (tema di italiano); students can choose from three different topics: one of them is usually related to literature, another to history, while the third may be a topical subject about current affairs (argomento di attualità).

The subjects of the second and third written assessments change depending on the schools; for instance, in the liceo classico, the subject of the second assessment is always Latin or Greek (students are required to do a versione, a translation from ancient Latin or Greek into Italian); in the liceo scientifico, the subject of the exam is mathematics, in the liceo linguistico, it’s a foreign language, etc.

Students who pass the written assessments are then permitted to sit the oral exam, which is based on a tesina. This is an interdisciplinary research, written by the student, covering different themes and subjects studied throughout their last year at school.

The examining board (commissione giudicatrice) is made up of six members; three of them are chosen from the school staff (membri interni) while the other three come from other schools (membri esterni). The head of the board is il presidente di commissione and is not a member of the school staff.

100 is regarded as full marks. In the case of outstanding performance, la commissione can give full marks plus a lode, reserved only for brilliant students.

The language and structures in the messages are very informal. Because of the nature of the context and audience, it may not always be correct and you will find that at times it’s quite colloquial.

To find out more about Italian teenage language go to http://147.162.119.4/linguagiovani/, select Dizionario LG.

... a qualche ora dall'esame sono qui a leggere i vostri messaggi che lo riguardano.

... la maturitá l´ho passata con 92.

In the two phrases above, the lo and l’ replace the nouns mentioned earlier in the sentence. This is to avoid repeating the same word. They are called i pronomi diretti.

Refer to Elementi linguistici – I pronomi diretti for a complete list of the direct object pronouns.

In Italia, usually there is a song associated with gli esami (di maturità) in a particular year. It does not necessarily mention exams but students have chosen it to be their exam theme song.

One of the songs mentioned here is Il giorno dei giorni by the artist Ligabue.

You can listen to the song on his official website www.ligabue.com by selecting discografia.

The song is featured on the album Nome e Cognome (2005). Select the song Il giorno dei giorni and listen to the music video clip.

To give you a better idea about this Italian teenage idol, you may wish to listen to some of the other songs available on this site.

If you have problems accessing this site, try a general search on www.yahoo.it, type Ligabue in the cerca box. Another alternative is to select Musica then search for Ligabue.
