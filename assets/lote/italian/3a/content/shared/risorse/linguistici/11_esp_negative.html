In Italian a negative expression is formed by simply putting the word non in front of the verb or the auxiliary (helping) verb in the passato prossimo.

The word non can also be used with other negative words.

In most cases the non will go before the verb and the negative word will go after.

Non ... niente

Non ... nulla 		nothing

not anything		 Non faccio niente di particolare il venerdì sera. I don't do anything special on Friday nights.

I do nothing special on Friday nights.
Non ... mai never

not ever		 Non faccio mai sport. I never do sport.

I don't ever do sport.
Non ... più no longer

not any longer		 Lorenzo non gioca più a calcio. Lorenzo no longer plays soccer.

Lorenzo doesn't play soccer any more.
Non ... affatto not at all Non ci piacciono affatto i film di fantascienza. We don't like science fiction films at all.
Non ... ancora not yet Non ci sono ancora risposte. There aren't any responses yet.
Non ... nessuno nobody

no-one

not anyone		 Loro non conoscono nessuno qui a Perth. They don't know anyone here in Perth.

They know no-one here in Perth.
Non ... neanche

Non … neppure

Non … nemmeno		 not even

neither		 Gli studenti non studiano neanche per gli esami. The students don't even study for exams.

The students don't study even for exams.
Non ... né ... né neither ... nor Non facciamo né calcio né tennis. We don't do (play) soccer or tennis.

We don't do (play) neither soccer nor tennis.
Non … mica not at all

not really		 Quei ragazzi non sono mica male! Those boys aren't bad at all!
The expressions are sometimes referred to as double negatives. This is because they contain two negative words.


Colloquially, it is common to drop the non in the double negative.

The phrase 'Non guardo mai la TV' in everyday conversation could be shortened to 'Guardo mai la TV'. This form is generally used only in colloquial spoken Italian.

The English 'nowhere' has no Italian equivalent and is best translated by the expression 'da nessuna parte' – which literally means 'in no place'.
