I pronomi relativi

Relative pronouns in English link two clauses (primary and subordinate) into one sentence. For example:

Questa è la mia amica che è appena arrivata dall’Italia.

There are two main ideas in this sentence:

  • 'this is my friend' (primary clause)
  • 'she has just arrived from Italy' (subordinate).

They are linked by the word 'who' and in Italian by 'che' which are relative pronouns.

The Italian relative pronouns are:

che who, whom,
that and which
  • it is invariable (only has the one form)
  • used to speak about people and things
  • it’s never used after a preposition.
 Come si chiama il ragazzo che ha vinto il concorso?
cui who, whom, that and which
  • used instead of che after a preposition
  • It’s invariable.
 Il ragazzo di cui ti ho parlato ieri, mi ha invitato al cinema.
il quale who or that
  • used with a definite article and agrees with the subject of the sentence
  • it changes to quali (i quali, le quali) if the noun it refers to is plural
  • this relative pronoun is not common in spoken Italian and, unless it is being used to avoid ambiguity, it will be replaced with either che or cui.
 Questo è l’iPod, il quale (che) ho prestato a Massimo.

Questi sono gli amici di Alessandra, i quali (che) sono in vacanza in Australia.
quello che that which (what)
  • can be shortened to quel che or ciò che.
 Non capisco bene quel che/quello che/ciò che spiega l’insegnante.
chi the one(s) who, he who, those who and whoever
  • has only one form and is often used in sayings or proverbs
  • Di chi is used as an interrogative and means whose.
 Chi parla poco, dice tanto.
Di chi è questo zaino?

Often in English the relative pronoun is omitted. For example:

Instead of 'I like the jacket that she is wearing',

we often say   'I like the jacket she's wearing'.

Note that the 'that' is missing from the second sentence.

  • In Italian the relative pronoun is never omitted.
  • Quale is also an interrogative adjective. For example:

Quale libro?

Quali studenti?
