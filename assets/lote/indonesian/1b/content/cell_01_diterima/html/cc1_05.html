Trish flicked a quick email to her friend Yanti telling her she'd been accepted for Tantangan Indonesia. She received the following reply.

Trish Barrett <trishgal@westone.wa.gov.au>

Yanti <julianti_smilez@westone.wa.gov.au>

Hai Trish,

'pa kabar? Yanti hepi sekali dengar kabar Trish ikut Tantangan Indonesia. Hebat! Kasih info lagi dong! Yanti ingin tahu gimana rencana Trish. Kapan Trish berangkat ke Indonesia? Berapa lama di Indonesia? Ke mana aja? Nginep di mana? Ayo, jelaskan dong.

Salam,

Yanti.

The context of communication is very important as it often means adapting the type of language you use. You are probably aware that you change the sort of language you use according to who you are speaking to and the situation. For example, in a job interview, you'd avoid using really slang expressions and saying some things that may be totally acceptable with your friends but not in an interview. You speak to your teacher differently than you do to your friends. When writing, you'd write an email to a friend differently to how you'd write a formal letter to someone you don't know. Whether you think about it or it comes naturally, you differentiate your communication according to the audience and situation. Click on the tabs on this box to discover some of the differences to be aware of in Indonesian.

Notice that Yanti uses her name (Yanti) for 'I' and Trish's name for 'you'. This is an indicator of familiarity, and it avoids any embarrassment that may be caused by using formal and distant terms (eg saya/Anda/Bapak/Ibu/Saudara/Saudari) or terms that may seem too familiar (eg aku/kamu/gue/lu). As Yanti is a friend of the same age, she could have used aku/kamu, but saya/Anda or a title would be too formal for this situation.

A good way to decide what terms of address to use in responding to a letter or email is to use the same sort of terms the original writer used. Looking at the personal pronouns (eg you/I) used in a letter gives you a good idea of the level of formality you should use in response.

Yanti uses some colloquialisms in her letter. She abbreviates Apa kabar by dropping the first letter and, similarly, bagaimana is shortened to gimana.

She also uses the casual expressions dong, and ayo. She also uses hepi, which is an example of English being adopted as slang because English is 'cool'. You would not use these sorts of expressions in a formal context.

Compare how this casual email from a friend differs from the formal letter you saw in the section Surat dari Tantangan Indonesia.

  • The formal letter starts with 'Kepada … yang terhormat'. It finishes with 'Hormat saya'.
  • The casual email starts with 'Hai Trish!' and finishes with 'salam'. Other good ways of starting a casual email or letter are:
    Kepada Trish yang baik, or just Trish yang baik.

Tulislah!

Reply to Yanti's email in Indonesian. Open the jadwal acara that was in Trish's information pack, which gives a rough itinerary of the trip.

Before you begin, prepare by doing the following.

  • Use your reading strategies to make sense of the jadwal acara.

    Revise the question words in the kosa kata section.

  • Note in English or Indonesian the questions you need to answer, and the information you will give.
  • Decide which opening and closing phrases and terms of address are appropriate.
  • Reply to Yanti's email. You should write approximately 100–150 words.
  • Resolve to open your dictionary only 3–5 times.

Remember, taking risks and having a go will really speed up and improve your language learning ability.

At this stage, don't worry too much about what you don't know, or 'getting it right'.

Also, try to communicate as much as you can in Indonesian, as simply as possible and using words you know.

The main thing is getting your meaning across.
