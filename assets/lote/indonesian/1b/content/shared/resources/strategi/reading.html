Strategi membaca (Reading strategies)

Reading can be daunting when there are unfamiliar words – whether they are in English or in a foreign language. When you are reading something in your first language that has a word you don't know (say a scientific text), you probably don't freak out, start crying and stop reading! Usually, you would just:

  • read past the word and try to guess its meaning; then
  • check the dictionary to see if you are right.

This is also a good strategy to use in Indonesian, even though there may be more words you don't know.

Other things you can do include the following.

Before you read

Ask yourself:

  • What do I know about the text before I start reading?
  • What do I know about the text type, pictures, and surrounding information?
  • What is the text-type?
  • Who is the text aimed at?
  • What is the purpose of the text?
  • What have I been told about the text?
  • What does the task say? What do I need to do or find out?

While you read

You should:

  • skim-read to get a general impression of themes, purpose and main topic, ignoring words you don't know
  • scan for key information such as the title, headings and subheadings and work out what they mean
  • use key words, cognates (words that are adapted from English) and the words before and after a word or phrase you don't know to deduce meaning
  • read a whole sentence, skipping over words you don't know and seeing if you can guess the meaning from the bits you do understand (this is called reading for gist)
  • highlight repeated words that you don't recognise and can't guess the meaning of
  • use your dictionary to look up key words. Check out the dictionary skills for tips on how to do this.

  • be sure the word is not a cognate
  • limit the words you look up to only key words that significantly alter the meaning of a sentence that you cannot guess.

Also, in Indonesian, remembering certain grammar points can help you. For example:

  • looking at the base-word is important so you can guess the meaning or find the word
  • you can often work out whether the word is a verb or a noun by looking at the affixes (for example, it starts with a me-, therefore it's a verb or doing word)
  • a word following a noun may be an adjective.

Remember:

  • you don't have to understand every word
  • don't worry, you can do it!

Reading strategy practice

  1. Choose a passage to read without referring to your dictionary.
  2. Take a minute to write down what you think the passage is about.
  3. Underline or highlight words you don't know and think are significant.
  4. Pick five of these words which you think are most important to understand more of the passage and underline them. Look them up now.
  5. Read the passage again. Were the five words useful in working out more of the meaning?
  6. Look up some other words. Were any of them more important?
