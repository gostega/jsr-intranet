The Chinese in Indonesia

The Chinese have a long history in Indonesia, dating as far back as the 15th century traders who came to the area. In colonial times the Dutch used the Chinese as the 'middlemen' between themselves and the colonised indigenous Indonesians, giving the Chinese jobs managing Indonesian workers. This sowed the seeds of some resentment between native Indonesians and the Chinese, who were better paid and had a higher status. The Dutch also forced the Chinese to live in designated areas, so they were not able to assimilate well into Indonesian society. The Chinese also had a reputation for being good at business and being able to progress economically, and this stereotype was promoted by Indonesian governments, creating further resentment. In the 1960s, the Indonesian Communist Party became strong enough to be a threat to the government. A communist coup took place and seven Indonesian generals were kidnapped and murdered, supposedly by the Partai Komunis Indonesia. The government took immediate action and hundreds of thousands of communists or suspected communists were massacred. This included many Chinese Indonesians. During the financial crisis of May 1998, Chinese Indonesians were also blamed for economic problems and targeted in riots. Even the term Cina has slightly negative connotations in Indonesian, so the more polite term to refer to someone of Chinese ethnicity is Tionghoa, or keturunan Tionghoa. To avoid persecution, many Indonesian-Chinese have Indonesianised their names and converted from their traditional religion to Islam or Christianity.
