Sentence structure

You will notice that there are several different sentence structures used in these questions. Don't panic â€“ it is not necessary to understand the sentence structure to understand the question. Remember the following:

  • If you can identify the question words and the key words, you can generally understand the gist of the question and attempt an answer.
  • If you don't understand a key word, you can use a repair strategy to seek clarification.

Look at the different ways of asking the same question:

  • Namanya siapa?
  • Siapa namanya?

The word order changes, but the question means the same thing. The key words and question words are the most important things.

The following example is more complex.

  • Keperluan pribadi apa yang harus saya bawa?
  • Apa keperluan pribadi yang harus saya bawa?
  • Saya harus (mem)bawa keperluan pribadi apa?

You can see that the question word changes its place in the sentence, but the meaning doesn't change much. Other things you may have noticed are:

  • The me- prefix can be dropped. In these examples bawa/membawa is used. This depends on the sentence structure and whether the word is written or spoken. In written language, prefixes are usually used, in spoken language they are often dropped.
  • The thing that is emphasised most comes at the beginning. (As a cultural influence, Indonesians often do not want to place the emphasis on themselves as it seems sombong or arrogant, so the word 'I' is used less as a sentence starter than it is in English.)

Bisa/boleh

Bisa and boleh are useful words to know in the context of asking questions, and are often combined with the suffix -kah to mean 'can I?' or 'may I?'

  • Bisakah saya beli vegemite di Indonesia?
  • Bolehkah saya bertanya?

The response to a 'can I' or 'may I' question is generally not ya or tidak, but bisa/boleh.
