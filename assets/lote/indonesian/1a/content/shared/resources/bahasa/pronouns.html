Pronouns

In English the words you and I are used a lot, and there aren't really any alternative words.

Indonesian has a number of ways of saying 'you' and 'I'. (These words can also mean 'yours' or 'my', depending on the syntax.)

I

saya formal, respectful
aku/ku casual, used between friends, teenagers
gue very slang form of 'I', used mostly by younger people and people in Jakarta
your name It sounds funny in English to say 'Laura wants to go to the movies tonight' instead of 'I want to go to the movies tonight', but it's perfectly normal in Indonesian.

You

Anda formal, respectful
kamu/mu casual, used between friends
engkau/kau less used, for very close relationships
lu very slang form of 'you', used mostly by younger people and Jakartanese
your name The forms of 'you' and 'I' used in a conversation can tell you something about the relationship between the participants – whether they are on an equal social footing or not, whether they are close or barely know each other – and about the situation (whether it is formal or informal for example).
You can also use a title such as Ibu or Bapak for older people, which has the double bonus of being extremely polite and also useful when you can't remember someone's name!

Note that the formal version of 'you' (Anda) has a capital letter, but unlike English, the word 'I' does not use a capital. This reflects the cultural value of others being more important than yourself.
