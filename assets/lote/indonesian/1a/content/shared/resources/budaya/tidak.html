Tidak!

Some of the auxiliary verbs you have learnt are used in questions and answers as well as in statements. Very rarely will you hear an Indonesian respond to a question with just 'Tidak'. Unlike English, in which you frequently hear people respond to questions with 'No', Indonesians usually give negative responses by saying things like 'can't', 'don't want to', 'not necessary', 'not yet'. There is even a rhyme suggesting that some of the most used phrases in Indonesian are:

tidak bisa can't
tidak mau don't want to
tidak usah not necessary
tidak tahu don't know

You will also see and hear the words belum and sudah used a lot more than in English.

For example, look at the following conversation between Tini and Steven:

Tini Sudah lancar bahasa Indonesia?
Steven Belum.
Tini Mau makan ikan?
Steven Tidak mau, terima kasih.
Tini Bisa makan sambal?
Steven Steven yang bayar, ya?
Tini Tidak usah. Siapa nama orang itu?
Steven Tidak tahu.

You may also have noticed in this conversation that the words 'you' and 'I' weren't used. In spoken Indonesian, if you are talking to someone and it is obvious you are referring to them or yourself, often the pronouns (words such as 'you', 'I', 'she', 'he', 'they') are dropped. For example, Tini says 'Mau makan ikan?', literally 'Want eat fish?', because it is unnecessary to say 'Do you …' as it is clear she means the person she is speaking to.
