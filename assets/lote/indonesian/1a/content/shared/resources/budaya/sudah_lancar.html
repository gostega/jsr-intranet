Wah, sudah lancar!

In some countries people don't like foreigners trying to speak their language, or speaking it imperfectly. The opposite is true in Indonesia. Indonesians are generally amazed, impressed and flattered that people from other countries are trying to learn their language. You may try out one or two words, and be greeted with amazement and phrases such as:

  • Wah, kok bisa bahasa Indonesia?
  • Lho, bisa bahasa Indonesia ya?
  • Wah, sudah pintar bahasa Indonesia.

In a culture that prides itself on modesty, it would be inappropriate to say, 'yeah, I'm pretty good at it, aren't I?' Instead, even if you speak like a native, it is polite to downplay your skills and say something like:

  • Oh, saya belum lancar; or
  • Bisa sedikit saja.

You're likely to then get a denial (of your denial) and be told how clever you are!

  • Sudah lancar. Pintar.

This is part of the culture of social interaction in Indonesia. While it may seem like a game, Indonesians do value other people making the effort to learn their language. A taxi driver in Lombok told an Australian who spoke Indonesian how clever she was, and said he himself was bodoh because he didn't speak English. She asked him which languages he did speak besides Indonesian, and he listed 'bahasa Sasak, bahasa Jawa, bahasa Sunda, bahasa Bali and a couple of other regional Indonesian languages. It is amazing to think someone who speaks six or seven languages can consider themselves bodoh.

Indonesians generally speak at least two languages. Most people speak a regional language first and bahasa Indonesia second, but it is not uncommon to speak more. The Balinese in particular, many of whom rely on tourism for their livelihood, have made particular efforts to learn foreign languages. Many Balinese have mastered several foreign languages, from English to Japanese to Russian to Korean.
