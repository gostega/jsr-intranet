General conventions and register

The register or formality of the language used depends on the magazine. You will see everything from very slang Jakartanese to very formal language used in Indonesian magazines.

There are several ways of opening and closing letters. The most common are explained below.

(Kepada) Dr Diego yang baik/Rizky yang baik-hati

Dear Dr Diego,
(Informal. Literally: ‘Dr Diego who is good/kind’. Using kepada meaning ‘to’ makes it slightly more formal.)

(Kepada) Dr Diego yang terhormat,

To Dr Diego who is most respected,
(Formal. To make it even more formal, you would use Bapak/Ibu.)

Salam (dari)

Regards (from)

Hormat saya

My respects/Yours sincerely

It is also normal in advice columns to sign off with your name, followed by your location (for example, town/city/province/island).  For example:

            Heru, Jayapura.

How would you sign off a letter?

Did you put: Name, Town?

Jangka waktu

Another common convention in advice letters is the use of words related to the jangka waktu of the masalah. Time indicators and seqeuencing words are used to explain when the problem started, how long it has been going on, and how the situation developed over time.

See the Pojok Bahasa for a range of time indicators.

Inti surat

The topic of the letter depends on the type of magazine. In teen magazines, the most popular topic is relationships with the opposite sex. Even in boys’ magazines such as Hai, letters tend to focus on romantic problems, particularly the problems of PDKT or pendekatan and nembak/tembak. Health, academic and friendship problems are secondary issues. In women’s magazines, advice letters are typically related to home issues – raising children, looking after a husband, health concerns, and other stereotypically ‘female’ issues. Other topics may relate to career issues or finances.

Due to the strong religious beliefs of many Indonesians and the prevalence of conservative Islam, it is fair to say that the Indonesian media is far more conservative than the Western media when it comes to discussing sex.  Despite the abolition of overt government censorship, there is still an expectation that the media will ‘guard society’s morals’, a part of which is considered to be ‘protecting’ people from references to and information about sex.  This attitude has also meant that sex-education in schools is limited at best. There are a number of Lembaga Swadaya Masyarakat (Non-government organisations, or community groups) that promote awareness of sexual health; however, these are usually small and under-funded groups with a huge number of people to reach.

As a result of this, Indonesian teenagers are often far less aware of the facts of life than Australian teenagers, and many myths relating to sex abound. There are some letters about sex in Indonesian advice columns, however, far less so than in Australian magazines, and the questions asked are often of the level that could be explained by primary-school age children in Australia. For example, an Australian exchange student was asked by a group of 19-year-old girls to explain what a tampon was, and whether it was true that using one means the girl is no longer a virgin. Another 19-year-old Indonesian girl came home in tears thinking she may be pregnant because her boyfriend insisted on kissing her on the lips while holding hands. Girls are expected to be virgins at marriage, whereas boys have very few restrictions placed upon them.

The number of teenagers engaging in sexual activity is increasing, but, in general, education about birth control is not considered necessary until after a married couple’s second child is born. Indonesian girls are not taught that they have the right to be assertive (for example, in saying no or insisting on birth control), especially in the more patriarchal ethnic groups. However, in an overpopulated society which is highly protective of its girls, teenage couples are rarely left alone. In girls’ boarding houses (or kos), there is usually a set 9 pm curfew, which is when the doors or gates are locked. Male visitors are not allowed beyond the front visiting room, and are usually supervised by friends of the girl or the boarding house ‘parents’. Male kos have no such rules and often do not even have a live-in supervisor.
