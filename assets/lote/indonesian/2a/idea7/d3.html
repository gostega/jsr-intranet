Superlatives

Paling or ter-

To say something is ‘the most adjective’, you can use either paling or the prefix ter-.

            Gedung yang paling tinggi di Malaysia adalah menara Petronas.

            Gedung yang tertinggi di Malaysia adalah menara Petronas.

Paling and ter- are technically interchangeable when you want to say something is ‘the most adjective’.  However, there are contexts in which one is more commonly used than another.

Paling is a little more frequently used in speaking. It is only used with adjectives (describing words). For example:

            Di mana tempat yang paling baik untuk berbelanja?

            Pada pendapat saya, makanan yang paling enak adalah mie goreng.

Ter- is more typically used in formal, written contexts. Formal letters (and envelopes) are usually addressed:

            Kepada X yang terhormat


Hati-hati!
Ter- does not always mean ‘the most’. It can be used with nouns (for example, terbuka) or adjectives (for example, tercantik). Sometimes it indicates:
            Accidental action – Saya tertidur.
            A state of being the base-word – Toko tertutup.

Occasionally, you may see both used for emphasis. For example:

            Dia orang yang paling terkenal di dunia musik.

Makin

Makin means ‘more’ and is often seen in advertisements, to communicate that something is ‘more’ adjective (better, bigger, yummier... ), a bit like the ‘-er’ on an adjective in English. It is similar to lebih; however, it communicates the idea that there is a process involved – something is becoming (or has become) more clean/delicious/white …

Makin bersih!
(Will/Has become) Cleaner!

Pakailah bumbu ini biar makin enak masakannya.
Use these spices so your cooking becomes yummier.

Makin bersih! Makin putih!
Cleaner! (making it become more clean); Whiter! (making it become more white).

Occasionally  you will see makin used twice, to indicate ‘the more (something happens) the more (something else happens)’.

Makin lama makin lancar bahasa Indonesianya.
As time goes on, his Indonesian gets more fluent. (Literally: more time, more fluent his Indonesian.)
