Dictionary skills

Use the right dictionary! Even the most experienced of us know the feeling ‘Hey! That word’s not in the diction–… oh! I’m in the wrong dictionary …’. Malu pasti, but it does happen.

Be aware of the conventions of your dictionary

Different dictionaries will have different explanations. Some examples include the following.

  • Often the noun form is the first word listed (eg bat – ‘a bat’ comes before ‘to bat’).
  • Abbreviations are often used (eg kb. means kata benda or noun. It is not the translation of the word you are looking for!).
  • Slashes are used to show you how to pronounce the word. They are not the translation! eg ‘Think/thingk/: pikiran’. The word for ‘think’ is pikiran; ‘thingk’ describes how to say the word ‘think’.
  • ‘Lih.’ is short for lihat which means ‘see’. So if you look up ‘threw’, it would say ‘lih. throw’ (see throw).

Finding the meaning of Indonesian words

Affixes are extremely important. By now, you should know not to look up a ber- word under ‘b’ (or a ‘me-‘ verb under ‘m’) but under the first letter of the base-word. Other prefixes include:

  • di-
  • pe- (plus peng, pem, peny, pen – added the same way as me- affixes)
  • ke- (usually there’s an -an at the end too)
  • ter-.

Finding the meaning of English words

Look up the base-word in English (for example, throwing/threw look up ‘throw’)

Choosing the correct word

You want to find out the Indonesian for the word ‘bat’. First, work out exactly the context you want to use the word in. Do you mean:

  • an implement used in cricket?
  • a small nocturnal flying mammal?
  • an action of hitting something (‘whose turn to bat?’)

What sort of word are you looking for? – are you looking for a noun, a verb, an adjective, something else? It gets tricky if you are looking for an English word that can be a noun or a verb – for example, drink. Are you looking for the action ‘to drink’, or the noun ‘a drink’? If you want to say ‘I drink water’ you need minum. If you want to say ‘that drink was nice,’ use minuman.

If it’s a verb, is it passive or active? I threw the ball = active = me-verb. The ball was thrown by Dave = passive = di-verb.

If there are multiple words listed in the dictionary (and there often are) look for the word that in context – ie in a sentence – that is closest to the meaning you want.
Look through the whole entry. If I want to say ‘she left him behind’, you will find that there may be an exact entry in the dictionary for ‘left behind’ I don’t want the word for ‘left-handed’, or ‘there were three left’, or ‘she left town’. The closest meaning may be something like ‘he left his bike at the gate’.
