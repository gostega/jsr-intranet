Listening and speaking strategies

General

  • It takes you a while to get used to someone’s voice – accent, mannerisms, style and pace of delivery – especially in a foreign language. If you are listening to a recording (for example, recorded message, song) don’t stress! Give yourself time to adjust to the speaker.
  • Listen for key words and words you already know.
  • Guess or infer meaning.

Note-making while listening

If you are listening to an audio text such as a radio report, make notes in Indonesian to help you recall and make sense of information. It is easier for your brain to process one language at a time when working with audio or audiovisual texts. Try the following strategy to see if it works for you.

  • Listen to the whole text, making notes in Indonesian as you do.
  • Go back over your notes, and make sense of what you have written down. Jot down key points in your first language.
  • Listen again and fill in any gaps and confirm your interpretation.

If you try to listen, interpret into English, then make notes while listening, you will probably find that the process is slower and you get left behind more quickly.

Conversation

Tarik nafas! Jangan gugup! Take a few deep breaths. Don’t be nervous.
Predict some questions you may be asked beforehand, and practise your answers aloud.

Listen for question words (see Pojok bahasa - Wawancara) as they tell you something about the question and response.

Appropriate language

Think about the register (or level of language) that is appropriate to the conversation, depending on your relationship with the speaker and the context. Especially consider which form of ‘you’ and ‘I’ you are going to use – Anda/saya, kamu/aku, lu/gue. NEVER use kamu with someone older than you or someone you don’t know very well. And remember, you can always ask!

Saya kurang pasti, apakah saya harus pakai istilah ‘Anda’ atau ‘Bapak/Ibu’, atau ‘kamu’, atau sesuatu yang lain. I don’t know if I should use the term Anda, or Bapak/Ibu, or kamu, or something else.

You may find it useful to use a disclaimer somewhere in the conversation. For example:

Maaf, saya kurang lancar bahasa Indonesia. Jangan tersinggung kalau saya berkata sesuatu yang kurang pas. I’m sorry, I’m not too fluent in Indonesian. Please don’t be offended if I say something that is not quite right.
Maaf, saya belum pintar bahasa Indonesia. Sorry, I’m not yet that good at Indonesian.

Repair strategies

Don’t be afraid to ask for help! Most native speakers are more than happy to help you out if they can. Of course, many people don’t speak English, so you need to be able to ask for (and cope with) help in Indonesian. Know a range of ‘repair strategies’ – phrases such as ‘please repeat’, ‘please slow down’, ‘what does (word) mean?’.

(Mohon) pelan-pelan (Please) speak slowly
Tolong ulangi! Please repeat
Apa artinya …? What is the meaning of …?
Saya tidak mengerti kata… Apa maksudnya? I don’t understand the word … What does it mean?
Anu, saya lupa katanya untuk … Umm, I’ve forgotten the word for …
Apakah maksud Anda …? Do you mean …?
Apa maksud Anda? What do you mean?
Apakah Anda bermaksud … Do you mean …?

Fillers

Fillers and particles of speech (see Pojok bahasa - Wawancara) are scattered throughout informal speech. Some of the most common include:

anu umm / what’s the word?
ya yes, you know?
nggak no / isn’t it?
‘kan will / not
Saya harus pikir sebentar dulu ya? Let me think a moment first, ok?

Expressions and exclamations

Expressions and exclamations can greatly enhance your communication.
