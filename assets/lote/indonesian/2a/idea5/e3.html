At a first glance, the last dot point on the sign looks like ‘please don't wear footwear other than shoes' but it makes a little more sense if we remember that sepatu are closed shoes, as opposed to 'sandal' which are thongs (or open shoes) and are considered more casual – inappropriate for school/uni/work ... As the kraton is a royal palace, correct and formal clothing, including closed shoes, is encouraged; however, there is some leniency for tourists. Signs in general in Indonesian are often not well observed – for example, you may see someone selling peanuts for the monkeys under the sign outside the Monkey Forest which proclaims ‘dilarang memberi makan hewan', or people bathing in the waterfall beyond the sign which states ‘dilarang mendekati air terjun'. However, shoes are a serious affair in Indonesia, and you should be aware of when to buka sepatu either by noticing a sign, or a collection of shoes, indicating that footwear should be removed when entering.
