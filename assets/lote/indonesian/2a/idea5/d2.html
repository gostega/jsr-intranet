-kan verbs

Me-kan verbs are often spoken and written differently in slang form. All you do is:

  • remove the me- (sometimes it’s replaced with a ng, or the ng- is left if it’s a meng- verb)
  • change -kan to -in.

For example:
            mengingatkan → ngingatin = to remind someone of something.

While -kan (as either me-kan or di-kan) has a few different functions, in all the cases above, the -kan makes the verb transitive – this means an object is required for the verb. For example, you can’t just mengucapkan you have to mengucapkan sesuatu. Another good example of a transitive verb in English is the word ‘hold’.

I am going to hold (transitive – requires an object) … a party/a sign/your hand.

In comparison, verbs like ‘swim’ are intransitive because you can say ‘I am going to swim’ without requiring any explanation of what you are going to swim.  Ber-verbs are like this in Indonesian, whereas -kan verbs are usually transitive and require an object.

Me-kan verbs also tend to be causative – they cause the object to take on the characteristics of the base-word. For example:

panas

hot

memanaskan

to heat

dingin

cold

mendinginkan

to chill

dekat

close

mendekatkan

to bring closer

If you know the base-word, you can often guess the meaning of the -kan word by thinking about what the word would be if it meant 'makes it (base-word)' or 'causes it to be (base-word)'.
