Kok gitu lho?

There are words in Indonesian that are used for emphasis, or just as meaningless parts of speech, that have become habit, for example using ‘like, you know’ in English (you know?).

There aren’t any hard and fast rules about when these are used – you just have to get a feel for when they are used by reading and listening to a lot of texts. Try using these words, but only in a casual spoken context, not in formal situations.

Kok – can be ‘why?’ at the beginning of a phrase; at the end of a phrase it just adds emphasis. (Remember the ‘glottal stop’ – you don’t quite sound the final ‘k’.)
            3_3_01.mp3 Kok begitu dong?

Lho – expression of surprise.
            3_3_02.mp3 Lho, kok mahal?

Deh – at the end of a phrase, similar to dong, indicating closeness.
            3_3_03.mp3 Nggak apa-apa deh.

Dong – similar to adding ‘mate’ to the end of a sentence.
            3_3_04.mp3 Tolongin aku dong!!

Gitu – from begitu, meaning ‘like that’.
            3_3_05.mp3 Kok gitu lho?

Sih – sih is another particle of speech that does not really mean anything.
            3_3_06.mp3 Nggak juga sih …

Pro-drop – often when speaking, personal pronouns (the words for you, I, he, she, they or it) can be left out. So ‘Kok gitu lho?’ could mean ‘why are you acting like that?’ or ‘why is s/he like that?’ or ‘why is the situation like that?’

‘kan – when you see ‘kan written, it can either be bukan or akan. It can be added to the end of a sentence to ask for a response, indicating that what you have said is right.

  • Kamu sudah mendapat SIM, kan? You’ve got your licence, haven’t you?
  • Saya boleh ikut, ya kan? I can come, can’t I? (Notice that sometimes a ‘yes/no’ ‘ya kan’ is added, indicating greater uncertainty.)
  • Kami harus serahkan tugas ini hari Jumat, ya kan? We have to hand this in on Friday, don’t we?

‘Belum’ can be used instead of ‘kan’ if you’re asking whether or not something has been done.

  • Sudah nonton film terbaru X-Men, belum?
  • Sudah serahkan PR, belum?

Indonesian is a fairly new language. Before the collection of islands now known as Indonesia became one nation, there was a great deal of work done to unify them. Bahasa Melayu was widely used around the Indo-Malay archipelago and was spread by sea-faring traders. It seemed to be an easy language to pick up, and already had some coverage in coastal areas, so it was adopted as Bahasa Indonesia in the early years of the twentieth century.

Over the years, Indonesian has both evolved and been deliberately developed into a separate language called bahasa Indonesia. There have been various efforts by the government at different stages in history to promote the use of bahasa Indonesia yang baik dan benar and to exclude the influence of foreign languages. Until 1998, the use of Chinese characters was banned, and the use of English words discouraged. Although most Indonesians speak a first language other than Indonesian (for example Javanese, or Balinese), Indonesian is the language of education, commerce and the media.
