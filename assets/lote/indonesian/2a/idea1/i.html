This section introduces you to the general features of majalah remaja Indonesia. With a population of over 220 million, the market for magazines in Indonesia is huge.

Klik di sini untuk membaca tentang majalah di Indonesia.

Even though magazines cost a fraction of what they do in Australia (around 12 000 rupiah), they are still a costly luxury beyond the financial capabilities of most Indonesians. (Check out http://www.xe.com for exchange rates.) As a result, there is an enormous market for magazines produced on newsprint rather than glossy paper, which are much cheaper. Online magazines or even television shows following a magazine format are also popular, as they cost nothing extra to those people who have access to a television or the Internet.

Magazines in Indonesia are produced mostly in Jakarta and other big cities like Yogyakarta and Surabaya, where the biggest markets are. Both the content of the magazines and the sort of language used, reflect the interests and language of people in these cities.  Therefore, it is important to realise that magazines are ‘Jakarta-centric’. There are attempts to represent and include people living outside Jakarta, but there is a definite Jakarta bias first, and secondly, a Java bias. (Java is the most densely populated island in the world.)

However, because the big media companies broadcast across all of Indonesia – television and radio having a broader reach and appeal than magazines – there is a radiating effect. The language and interests represented in the media have spread throughout other areas of Indonesia, so slang phrases that used to be heard only in Jakarta are now heard all over Indonesia. Slang varies from region to region (just as Australian English has different colloquialisms from UK English, American English, or South African English) and with over 700 different languages spoken throughout Indonesia, there is a huge variety of regional slang.

This topic covers the basics of colloquial Indonesian that is used in magazines and other popular media, and by teenagers and close acquaintances in their 20s and 30s. Without a knowledge of the basics of Indonesian slang, it can be difficult to read or understand popular texts and everyday conversation. However, you do not necessarily have to be able to speak slang yourself. It is important to know when and where it is appropriate to use slang. For example:

  • Spoken Indonesian is different from written Indonesian. (This is not too difficult – in English, you write a bit differently than you speak, ‘donchya’?)
  • The language you use differs according to the context of communication. (The context of communication refers to both the situation in which the communication occurs and the type of communication. For example, you speak to your teacher differently than you do to your friends; you use different language writing an essay to the language you use when writing an SMS to your friends.)

Outcomes

  • You will be able to recognise and understand some common bahasa gaul words.
  • You will be able to differentiate between bahasa gaul and bahasa baku.
  • You will be able to choose the appropriate register (the level of language used, such as formal or informal) for a situation.
  • You will consolidate strategies for reading texts.
  • You will form an impression of the interests and identity of Indonesian teenagers.

You will engage with the following text types:

  • magazine covers
  • contents pages
  • SMS.
