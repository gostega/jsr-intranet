Salam pembukaan

When you open a telephone conversation, you generally greet the person at the other end, say who is calling, who you wish to talk to, and so on. If the person you wish to speak to does not pick up the phone, you may ask 'Boleh bicara dengan ...?’ or 'Bisa bicara dengan ...?’ . You may also be asked 'Dari siapa?’ or 'Dari mana?’

Basa basi

If you are making a personal phone call (or holding an informal conversation in person), unless it is an emergency, it is fairly usual to make small talk (basa basi) first, rather than getting straight to the point. This can be as simple as asking 'Apa kabar?’. Between close acquaintances, it is also common to ask, 'Sudah makan?’ or 'Sudah mandi?’

Penjelasan / Tujuan

After you have opened with some pleasantries, you can get to the point of the conversation. If the person you want to speak to is not available, you can titip pesan. Two very useful words are begini and begitu. Begitu can also be used as a question with the right tone of voice, meaning 'oh, it’s like that is it?’. For emphasis you would say, 'mmm. begini lho.’

Penutupan

Finally, the penutupan . It is polite to thank the person you are speaking to, or to pass on your best wishes to others by saying 'salam buat’ or 'salam pada’. Casual conversations often close with the slang 'daag’ (pronounced 'da’). In Java, the word mari is often used in closing a conversation on the phone or in person.

You can find a full list of phrases useful for telephone conversations in the Pojok Bahasa.


Question tags are often used in speech to prompt some reaction from the other person. In Australian English, phrases such as, 'you know?’, 'don’t you think?’, 'right?’ or 'okay?’ are added on to the end of statements. Sometimes, these emphasise that a question is being asked, but sometimes they are just used to prompt the person you are speaking with to agree with you or acknowledge that they have heard what you said. In Indonesian, you will most often hear question tags such as 'ya’, 'nggak’, 'kan (bukan), or a combination of 'ya’ and a negative (for example, ya kan? or Ya nggak?) used.
