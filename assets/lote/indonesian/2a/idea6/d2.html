Forms of address

In English, when speaking to someone, you basically have only the terms 'I’ and 'you’ to refer to yourself and the other person. However, in Indonesian there are a number of ways of saying 'I’ and 'you’. It is very important to use the right terms so you do not cause offence. Forms of address differ according to the context or situation of the conversation, and the person you are addressing and the status of the relationship.

You may also hear the terms Bunda (Ibu) and Ayah (Bapak) used as highly respectful terms of address for parents.

Sapaan pakai nama?

In Indonesian, you can use your name to mean 'I’ and the other person’s name to mean 'you’. For example,

Kenalkan, nama saya Nora. Kemarin, Nora pergi ke toko dan membeli oleh-oleh untuk teman Nora di Indonesia. Oleh-oleh itu akan Nora bawa pulang.

It is difficult to get used to this from an English speaker’s perspective - try talking about yourself using your name instead of 'I’. It sounds strange in English, but it is perfectly normal in Indonesian. It’s also a good way of remembering people’s names - asking them a few questions first using their name instead of 'Anda’ and it means you don’t need to worry about deciding whether to use 'Anda / kamu / lu’!

Relationship Implications

While it is quite simple to use 'saya/Anda’ for a formal conversation, and 'aku/kamu’ for a less formal conversation, they imply something about the relationship between the speakers. For example, 'saya/Anda’ implies that you are not on close terms with the person with whom you are speaking, whereas 'aku/kamu’ implies a closer relationship.

Things get a little more complicated when the terms of address are mixed up. For example, the combinations below can suggest the following

  • saya + Anda = we are equal but on a formal basis / I don’t know you very well. This is the most acceptable set of pronouns to use.
  • saya + kamu = I am more important than you
  • aku/Nama + Anda/Bapak/Ibu = you are more important than me, I am showing you respect
  • Nama + Nama = we’re close / I’m not sure what to call you.

As a non-background speaker of Indonesian, people realise that you are likely to make mistakes, and therefore it is not a huge issue if you do. Indonesians are generally so pleased that you are attempting to speak their language that they will overlook mistakes unless they genuinely can not understand. Terms of address are something to be sensitive to and to be aware of, but don’t stress too much over it. Some suggestions are:

It is better to be too polite than not polite enough. If you are unsure, use Anda/Bapak/Ibu until you are invited to use a less formal term.

You can explain: 'Hanya ada istilah 'you’ dan 'I’ dalam bahasa Inggris, sedangkan dalam bahasa Indonesia ada kamu/Anda/lu/ dan seterusnya jadi saya bingung’.

Beginning formal speeches (such as in public forums or to visitors) with apologies is very common and culturally appropriate. Even if your Indonesian is outstanding, it is polite to play it down. 'Maaf, saya kurang lancar/fasih bahasa Indonesia. Jangan tersinggung kalau saya berkata sesuatu yang kurang pas’.

You can always ask! 'Saya kurang pasti, apakah saya harus pakai istilah 'Anda atau Bapak/Ibu, atau kamu, atau sesuatu yang lain. Saya belum pintar bahasa Indonesia’.
